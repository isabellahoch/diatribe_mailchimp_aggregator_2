In his latest piece for diaTribe, Jim Hirsch dives into Type 1 Team Texas’s work during Hurricane Harvey. Jim tells the story of a heroic group of Texas residents who distributed insulin vials, insulin pens, meters, test strips, infusion sets, CGM sensors, pods, glucagon, syringes, and more essential supplies to people with diabetes affected by the hurricane. Though Harvey has dissipated over Texas, the Type 1 Team’s work continues as the hurricane’s effects ripple throughout local communities.  

On a lighter note, two adjunctive treatments for type 1 diabetes were big news at the European Association for the Study of Diabetes (EASD) meeting in Lisbon last week. The InTandem3 and DEPICT 1 trials examined the efficacy of sotagliflozin and Farxiga, two oral medications that treat type 1. Though both studies found the drugs to confer A1c and weight loss benefits, there are residual concerns about the elevated DKA risk found in each trial. Importantly, our coverage of these trials should not be considered a head-to-head comparison of the two drugs but rather an overview of the exciting possibilities offered by this new class for people with type 1.

Additionally on the drug front – the FDA recently added a new heart failure warning to the labels of Januvia, a DPP-4 inhibitor, and Janumet, the same DPP-4 inhibitor combined with Metformin. Though a cardiovascular outcomes trial found that Januvia had a neutral effect on the heart, it seems as though the FDA is taking a conservative approach to the entire class of drugs. 

Finally, Health Canada has just approved Tresiba, a once-daily basal insulin for adults with type 1 and type 2. With an expected launch in October, Tresiba is poised to make a meaningful difference for Canadians who are concerned about hypoglycemia or enjoy the convenience of flexible dosing.
