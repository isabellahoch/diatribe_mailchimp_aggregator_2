Greetings from San Francisco! Thank you all for being with us just under one month ago to discuss “supercharging” wellness and prevention. The diaTribe Foundation learned so much that will impact people affected by diabetes and prediabetes, and the room walked away with unquantifiable collective wisdom about chronic disease.
 
To further these learnings, we’d like to share some of the high-level takeaways from the breakfast:
  • There is a huge discrepancy between diabetes prevalence and awareness, particularly in minority populations. Prevalence in the African American population is 36% compared to 10% awareness, and prevalence in the Hispanic population is 34% versus 7.5% awareness. This lack of awareness translates into minimal prevention – very few people with diabetes, diabetes caregivers, or healthcare professionals know about the DPP, for example. To raise awareness, participants agreed that we must form partnerships between HCPs, healthcare organizations, and community members and encourage everyone to think outside their own sphere. 
  • Medical education about chronic disease, particularly diabetes, is failing. In medical schools, students receive minimal lecturing on diabetes and training in outpatient settings. How will HCPs feel comfortable engaging patients in lifestyle and behavior change? Perhaps they, along with the small percentage of time they spend with patients, are not the solution. To encourage lifestyle change, participants suggested leveraging professionals outside of the physician workforce who have more time to work directly with patients.
  • Evidence-based quality measures of diabetes care need to shift towards prevention. Many of the incentives as they stand are focused on treatment outcomes like A1C. What if we could shift gears to focus on prevention – how many are enrolled in the DPP, how many are screened? This reimbursement for prevention will go a long way.
  • The diabetes epidemic necessitates a systems-level approach. Diabetes prevention is the responsibility of the entire community. Policies, hospitals, institutions, cities, states, and small and large employers must join hands in tackling prediabetes and diabetes. Then, we can begin to develop and scale programs that really move the needle on diabetes prevention and care.
 
And speaking of supercharging prevention, we were elated to have not one but TWO editorials (on Morning Consult and The Aspen Times) recently published on The Anthology of Bright Spots, a unified collection of successes in type 2 diabetes prevention and management. If you haven’t had a chance to peruse the Anthology’s insights, there’s no time like the present!

Who can you reach out and forget a connection with? How can we generate, together, the greatest impact on diabetes and prediabetes?
