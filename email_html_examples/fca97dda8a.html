2. ADVOCACY

Dexcom FDA Hearing: There was a very successful day at the FDA on 7/21, where the FDA advisory panel voted 8-2 in favor of approving the Dexcom G5 CGM for dosing insulin. To prepare for the event, TdF wrote a letter to the FDA detailing the scientific rationale behind our support of the label update. We collected ~10,000 signatures and over 1,000 comments on the letter, which we submitted to the FDA the week before the hearing. Adam put together a terrific video that we shared with our networks to spread the word about the hearing and garner additional signatures. We also hosted dinner for nearly half of the 35 speakers at the meeting’s Open Public Hearing (OPH), and provided explicit advice on 10 presentations; Adam, Ava, and Kelly all shared testimonies as well. In addition, we got permission from the FDA to Facebook Live Stream the event during the OPH and panel vote – over 2,000 tuned in to our live coverage! After the event, we wrote a post for Kelly’s blog, a Closer Look report, and will also have an article on the event in our next diaTribe issue! 

8/29 FDA Workshop on Outcomes Beyond A1c: TdF collaborated closely with JDRF, ADA, and AACE to organize this FDA-sponsored public workshop. The goal of the event was to discuss, identify, and qualify diabetes outcomes measures beyond A1c to help inform future regulatory processes. TdF collaborated with HCM to create a video to bring as many diverse patient voices to the FDA as possible. The video played during the event itself, and featured a collection of patient responses to the following questions:
  • What I struggle with most in managing my diabetes is _______. 
  • Beyond helping me with A1c, I wish a new diabetes drug would ______. 
View the video here.

Following the meeting we immediately published  a follow up article in diaTribe that reached nearly 100,000 people. In addition, we provided everyone at the FDA with a follow up reference booklet containing patient comments and material useful when looking beyond A1c. We are hopeful this will spark increased engagement on this critical issue. Notably, nearly half the comments in the book related to access although we let patients know FDA did not control this. For this and other reasons, we continue to want to begin work on access.
