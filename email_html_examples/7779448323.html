1. South by Southwest (SXSW) Panels:

Out of over 400 proposals submitted, I am honored that two of The diaTribe Foundation’s panel proposals were accepted for the SXSW Conference in March in Austin, Texas and these panels took place yesterday and today - as SXSW leaders have shared with us, it’s very unusual to have two accepted and the speakers did a standout job! The following panels were inspired by your ideas in the Consensus of Ideas.
  • A #)$%(*% Crisis: Training the Newest Gen of Docs, inspired by Idea #12, this took place earlier today, and featured d16’s Bon Ku of Thomas Jefferson University as well as Dr. Erin Kane of Johns Hopkins Hospital, Dr. Sarah Kim of UCSF, and Dr. Susan Cox of Dell Medical School at UT Austin. Here is our article on diaTribe.org published earlier this evening! 
  • How Do We Prevent a Diabetes Avalanche, inspired by Idea #12: Investing in the Health of People, Companies, and Cities, took place yesterday, March 12, and it featured d16’s Dr. Sarah Mummah of IDEO and d16's Adam Brown of The diaTribe Foundation, in addition to Dr. Phyllisa Deroze of Black Diabetic Info and Marie Schiller, VP of Drug Delivery/Device R&D Innovation at Eli Lilly's Cambridge Innovation Center. d16's Dave deBronkart helped us get on this panel, and he was going to be on the panel himself, except that he had a conflict! Here is the piece on diaTribe.org that the world will see tomorrow! 
We have been excited to be at SXSW to shine a light on chronic disease and, specifically, on diabetes and pre-diabetes, and to showcase some of the brilliant minds who contributed to d16. Although the planners did not allow us to live broadcast the panels on Facebook Live, we did tape them. Please let me know if you would like a link of the panels once uploaded; this will surely be an invaluable opportunity to increase public awareness of many of the ideas and initiatives discussed at d16.
 

2. The Anthology of Bright Spots:

In the few months since I sent you the initial collection of Anthology program summaries, we’ve reached the following milestones:
  • We have conducted nearly 60 interviews (many with those of you who were willing to share your insights);
  • We have screened and researched an additional 40 prevention efforts and had informal conversations with dozens more;
  • We attended the 9th World Congress on the Prevention of Diabetes and its Complications in Atlanta, where we saw many presentations and met with many more physicians, policymakers, and scientists;
  • We began working with the designers who will assist us with the presentation of the Anthology --> picture a fabulous, engaging website;
  • And, of course, our biggest milestone to date – the first complete draft of The Anthology of Bright Spots in prevention of type 2 diabetes.
Our efforts to improve this Anthology will continue as we work to conduct more interviews, and create a navigable online version of this report. I would love your thoughts on: How can we strengthen this project as a whole and improve its presentation? What would you add? It's very American-based right now, and we’d love to add more international programs. Do you know of any fantastic international examples?  Please email us with your thoughts, and if you would like a copy in Word to mark up, just let me know. Our next foray for the Anthology inspired by Dr. James Gavin and other leaders is "workplace wellness" followed by "healthcare teams of the future," most likely. Let us know your thoughts!

Since our last update, we have also been busy at diaTribe.org and The diaTribe Foundation. In addition to the projects just discussed and our record development work, we've made strides on the regulatory and education fronts as well. And, in expanding our capacity!

-- First, on education, we are now at over 100,000 subscribers at diaTribe.org - this was our "stretch" goal for the end of 2016, and we're thrilled we achieved this milestone! Indeed, we're thinking all the time about how to engage readers and amplify patient voices. I was excited when the FDA responded positively to a key indication that diaTribe had requested of the FDA last summer, one in which  10,000 diaTribe readers had signed  - kudos to Adam Brown and Ava Runge, who is the head of diaTribe’s advocacy, for leading this effort.  Our diaTribe.org managing editor, Lynn Kennedy, has led the efforts on attracting and engaging readers. She’s already made significant inroads, but  if you have any ideas for her or thoughts on great writers for diaTribe, please be in touch.  

-- Second, last year we had considerable success in amplifying patients’ voices with the FDA. I'm proud of a 3,500-patient survey we presented there in the summer (complete with 2,676 open-ended patient comments); we commissioned the survey from our sister organization dQ&A. We also submitted a letter to the FDA, asking that it listen more closely to patient requests (diaTribe requested key changes to regulatory guidance). We were happy to hear from Dr. Janet Woodcock, director of FDA’s center for Drug Evaluation and Research, last month - she requested a 30-minute call with us, which we had in early February, and now we are looking forward to gathering multiple entities for a meeting this summer. We'll be co-chairing a session on CGM standardization at the ADA Scientific Sessions in June, and will be showing that data in Washington DC shortly thereafter. Last, we hit the "send" button at 1:50 pm PST to submit that data today as a late-breaker submission. Ten minutes before deadline - not bad! Thank you to Close Concerns for help on getting that done.
