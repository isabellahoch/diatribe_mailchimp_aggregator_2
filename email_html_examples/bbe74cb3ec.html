I hope you have had a wonderful January and start to the year! I am writing with a special “insider look” into some of our top diaTribe articles from January. I have also included some sample tweets at the bottom of this letter – thank you so much for your support in sharing these articles.

1. How to Get Diabetes Drugs for Free: In our last article in the diaTribe Series on Access, we provide a comprehensive list of Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs), which help people who meet certain eligibility requirements get diabetes drugs for free. Our article is based on six-month-long research into these programs, and breaks down current PAPs in the US by manufacturer to help people find the program right for them. Plus, we give an overview of what a PAP application looks like, what the requirements are, and tips to complete the application with healthcare providers. Read about other topics related to access, like insurance appeals, employee rights at work, making the switch to Medicare with diabetes, and saving money on diabetes care here.

 
2. Medicare Now Covers Abbott’s FreeStyle Libre CGM: Just a few months after FDA approval, people over 65 with type 1 or type 2 diabetes can now receive Medicare coverage of Abbott’s FreeStyle Libre. To learn more about the Medicare coverage of FreeStyle Libre and whether any other CGM systems could secure Medicare coverage this year, click here. 
 
3. Adjusting Insulin Based on CGM: New Guidelines from Endocrine Society: The Endocrine Society recently released new recommendations for adults and children for adjusting insulin dosing based on Dexcom G5 CGM trend arrows. These guidelines take an individual’s insulin sensitivity into account and include tables that summarize adjustment guidelines for insulin dosing. Read our article for more background on these guidelines and how to use these recommendations to identify the most appropriate dose adjustment.
 
4. FDA Approves Admelog, the First Biosimilar Mealtime Insulin: The FDA recently approved Admelog, the first biosimilar mealtime insulin. This rapid-acting insulin is a “follow-on” to the common mealtime insulin Humalog, meaning it uses the same type of insulin and is just as safe and effective. Though biosimilar long-acting (basal) insulins have been approved previously, Admelog is the first approved rapid-acting (mealtime) biosimilar insulin.
